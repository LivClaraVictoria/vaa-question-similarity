# Can Questionnaire Manipulation Distort Voting Advice? Clone-Robust Weighting as a Defense for VAAs

**Keywords:** Voting Advice Applications, Clone-Robust Weighting, Questionnaire Manipulation, Sentence Embeddings, Democratic Technology

---

## Extended Abstract

Voting Advice Applications (VAAs) have become an integral part of democratic processes, helping millions of voters navigate complex political landscapes by matching their policy preferences to candidates [1]. SmartVote, Switzerland's leading VAA, recorded over 1.8 million uses during the 2023 National Council elections alone. These platforms compute voter–candidate distances across a fixed questionnaire, treating each question with equal weight. However, this design creates a structural vulnerability: if certain policy topics are overrepresented in the questionnaire—whether through editorial imbalance, lobbying, or deliberate manipulation—they receive disproportionate influence over recommendations.

We formalize this threat by systematically cloning questions and measuring the resulting recommendation distortion. In an exhaustive sweep across all 75 questions in the SmartVote 2023 questionnaire, we clone each question 10 times and compare the resulting voter recommendations against the original. The distortion is substantial and universal: the worst-case question (on retirement age) causes 46% of all voter recommendations to change (Jaccard similarity = 0.54), and even the least impactful question still alters 14% of recommendations (Jaccard = 0.86). On average, duplicating any single question changes 29% of recommendations. Notably, the most vulnerable questions are those that elicit the most divisive responses, as measured by answer variance (Spearman ρ = 0.70, p < 0.001).

To address this vulnerability, we apply Clone-Robust Weighting (CRW) [2], which assigns each question a weight inversely related to its local density in a distance space—questions surrounded by many close neighbors are downweighted as redundant, while isolated questions retain full influence. CRW depends critically on a distance metric that recognizes question variants—paraphrases and negations of the same question—as close, regardless of surface phrasing. We evaluate 10 sentence embedding models and find that CRW effectiveness varies by up to 31.5% depending on model choice, raising the question of which embedding properties make a model suitable for CRW. Our contributions are: (1) the first empirical evaluation of CRW on real-world VAA data, benchmarking 10 embedding models across 6 clone types; (2) a demonstration that strategic cloning of just 5 questions can shift a target party's visibility by +4.76 percentage points; and (3) evidence that CRW reduces this manipulation effect by 22–38% when paired with appropriate embedding models.

**Data and methods.** We use data from the SmartVote platform for the 2023 Swiss National Council elections, focusing on the canton of Zurich. The dataset comprises 75 policy questions answered by approximately 1,100 voters and over 600 candidates on a 4-point Likert scale. Each voter receives a ranked list of their top-36 candidate recommendations, matching Zurich's number of parliamentary seats. We first identify worst-case questions by cloning each of the 75 questions individually (10 identical copies), injecting the clones in-memory, and recomputing recommendations. We compare cloned against original recommendations using Jaccard similarity (top-36 set overlap), Spearman ρ, and Kendall τ, ranking questions by a composite score. The top 5 most impactful questions—spanning retirement policy, media regulation, immigration, gender identity, and healthcare—are selected for the main experiment. For each, we generate 4 synthetic clones using GPT-4o paraphrases, yielding 6 experimental conditions: easy paraphrase, hard paraphrase (no shared content words), negation combined with easy paraphrase, negation combined with hard paraphrase, a mixed condition (2 easy + 2 negation-easy), and a balanced mix of all types. This produces 95 questions per condition (75 original + 20 clones). CRW integrates question weights over a distance threshold parameter α, which we sweep across 21 values from 0.01 to 3.0. We test 10 multilingual sentence embedding models spanning standard STS baselines (SBERT) to recent instruction-tuned and task-aware architectures (E5-Instruct, Jina v3, Qwen3, among others). To assess democratic consequences, we simulate a strategic manipulation targeting the Centre party ("Die Mitte"). Using a per-question party visibility sweep, we identify the 5 questions whose cloning most benefits Centre, then clone them simultaneously under a worst-case scenario (4 mixed clones per question, 20 total) and a realistic scenario (1 easy paraphrase per question, 5 total). Party visibility is computed using the Swiss government proportional method: each voter's top-36 recommendations are distributed across candidates' parties and aggregated across all voters.

**Results.** Our question impact sweep confirms that duplicating any question meaningfully distorts voter recommendations. The 5 most impactful questions span distinct policy domains, demonstrating that the vulnerability is structural rather than topic-specific. Clone impact correlates strongly with answer variance (ρ = 0.70, p < 0.001) but not with inter-question answer correlation (ρ = −0.14, p = 0.25), suggesting that the divisiveness of a question—not its redundancy with other questions—determines how much cloning distorts recommendations. Across all 6 clone conditions, the best-performing model (E5-Instruct) achieves a mean peak Jaccard similarity of 0.580—a 31.5% improvement over the unweighted baseline of 0.441. The worst-performing model achieves only 0.483. To investigate what drives model performance, we construct a synthetic benchmark in which models must place rephrased and negated variants of a question closer to the original than unrelated questions that share syntax or keywords. Performance on this benchmark—ordering accuracy—correlates significantly with CRW effectiveness (Spearman ρ = 0.648, p = 0.042), suggesting that models which better recognize question variants regardless of phrasing provide more effective CRW correction. However, one notable outlier (SBERT) achieves above-median CRW correction despite poor benchmark performance on negations, indicating that no single embedding property fully explains CRW effectiveness. In the worst-case party attack scenario (20 mixed clones of 5 Centre-benefiting questions), Centre's visibility share jumps from 17.45% to 22.21% (+4.76 percentage points), while left-wing parties SP and Green each lose approximately 2.4pp—equivalent to shifting roughly 5 of 36 Zurich seats. CRW with E5-Instruct (α = 0.3) reduces Centre's gain to +3.72pp, a 22% reduction of the attack effect. In the realistic scenario (5 easy paraphrases), the attack shifts Centre by +1.83pp, and CRW achieves a 38% reduction. The stronger correction for realistic clones reflects their tighter clustering in embedding space, making them easier for CRW to detect.

**Discussion.** Our results show that CRW can partially mitigate the effects of questionnaire manipulation, but substantial residual distortion remains even with the best-performing model and optimally tuned α. CRW effectiveness varies considerably across embedding models, clone types, and attack scenarios—and the degree of correction depends on choices (model, α) for which we lack principled selection criteria. Several limitations warrant discussion. CRW correction is partial: it reduces but does not fully eliminate the manipulation effect. Hard paraphrases that avoid sharing content words with the original are more difficult for all models to detect. CRW also operates asymmetrically—it effectively downweights overrepresented topics but cannot meaningfully compensate for underrepresented ones. Finally, our analysis is based on a single election (Zurich 2023); generalization to other VAA systems, languages, and political contexts requires further investigation. Promising extensions include deploying CRW as a real-time auditing tool during questionnaire design—flagging suspiciously clustered questions before they influence voters—and combining embedding-based distances with answer-correlation metrics for a richer notion of question redundancy.

Clone questions (red) receive weights between 0.35 and 0.55, while original questions (blue) retain weights near 1.0, demonstrating that CRW automatically identifies and downweights redundant questions without supervision.
